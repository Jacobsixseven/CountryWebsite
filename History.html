George Washington's journeys, from Revolutionary battlefields like the Delaware crossing to his presidential tours,
were significant for forging national identity, demonstrating leadership, and uniting a new republic through personal presence
and bold action. These travels transformed abstract governance into a tangible connection, solidifying the people's faith in the
federal government and showcasing his commitment to a strong, unified nation during its fragile infancy.

"We the People" signifies that governmental power originates from the citizens, not rulers, establishing popular sovereignty as
the bedrock of U.S. democracy, and it also declares that "the People" collectively ordain the Constitution to form a more perfect
Union for everyone, not just a select few. The phrase underscores that the government's authority is derived from the consent of the
governed and serves the whole populace, evolving to include all individuals as the nation has matured.

Uncle Sam signifies the U.S. government and national identity, evolving from a symbol of unity to a powerful call for patriotism,
famously seen in recruitment posters urging citizens to support the nation, especially during wartime. He embodies American power,
duty, and the collective spirit, representing everything from military enlistment and taxation to the broader American ethos of
liberty and democracy.

The Boston Massacre was significant as powerful Patriot propaganda, fueling anti-British sentiment by portraying British tyranny
and igniting revolutionary fervor by showing troops firing on civilians, a pivotal step toward the American Revolution. It
crystallized colonial outrage, demonstrating a perceived abuse of power that united disparate colonists and laid groundwork for
independence, as patriots used it to rally support for separation.
